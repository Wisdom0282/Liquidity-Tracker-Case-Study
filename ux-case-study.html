01 Problem

Finance teams didn't know where their money was

Enterprise sellers operating across EMEA markets — managing inventory in Germany, payouts to UK suppliers, and VAT obligations in France — had no unified view of their liquidity position. Every Friday, a finance manager at a mid-size marketplace would spend 2–3 hours manually reconciling five separate tools: a logistics platform, a bank portal, a VAT compliance SaaS, a currency exchange spreadsheet, and an internal order management system.

The Core Problem Statement

"I have money somewhere in the system. I don't know if it's stuck in customs, held by the bank, or waiting on a VAT reclaim. I only find out when something breaks — a supplier payment fails, a penalty arrives, or a shipment is delayed."

This wasn't a reporting problem. It was a situational awareness problem. The data existed — it was fragmented across systems that didn't speak to each other. The finance team was operating blind between reconciliation cycles.

The cost of not knowing was measurable: penalty fees for overdue VAT filings, shipments sitting at customs for 11+ days because no one had flagged a documentation error, and delayed supplier payments straining relationships with EMEA partners.

Business Impact

Late VAT filings, missed payout windows, and customs delays were creating compounding costs estimated at 3–4% of monthly GMV in avoidable friction.

User Impact

Finance managers reported high cognitive load and anxiety around month-end reconciliation. "I go into it knowing something will be wrong — I just don't know what."

02 Research

Understanding the actual workflow

Before designing anything, I spent two weeks in discovery — conducting contextual interviews and workflow shadowing with 8 finance professionals across 4 EMEA-focused marketplace businesses (2 SME, 2 mid-market). The goal was not to validate a solution but to understand the mental model of cross-border money management.

Week 1–2
Contextual Interviews & Workflow Shadowing
8 participants · 90-min sessions · Recorded screen sessions during reconciliation cycles. Key method: "Show me the last time money was stuck — walk me through exactly what you did."
Week 2–3
Affinity Mapping & Jobs-to-be-Done Framework
Synthesised 340 data points into 6 primary jobs. Identified 3 critical jobs that weren't being served: real-time block visibility, root-cause attribution, and urgency ranking.
Week 3
Competitive Audit
Reviewed 6 tools: Airwallex, Wise Business, Payoneer, Netsuite, Stripe Treasury, Brex. None solved the cross-system aggregation problem. All optimised for the transaction layer, not the operational awareness layer.
Week 3–4
Domain Expert Sessions
3 sessions with a VAT compliance specialist and 2 EMEA logistics operations leads. Critical learning: customs holds and tax delays follow predictable patterns — they're not random, they're navigable if surfaced early.

"The problem isn't that the data doesn't exist. The problem is that by the time I've found it, it's too late to act on it without a scramble."

— Finance Manager, mid-market EMEA marketplace, Interview #4

The most significant research finding was about mental model mismatch. Finance managers didn't think about their money in terms of currencies or accounts. They thought about it in terms of states: money that was safe, money that was moving, and money that was stuck. Every existing tool was organized by transaction type. The mental model was organized by urgency and control.

Critical Research Insight

Users had developed an informal triage system — Post-it notes on monitors, a "red tab" in their spreadsheet, a weekly Slack message to logistics. They were manually building the dashboard that didn't exist. Our job was to make that invisible system visible.

Key Jobs-to-be-Done Identified

J1

Know immediately if something needs my attention today — without running a reconciliation. The "first 60 seconds of the day" job.

J2

Understand why money is stuck, not just that it is. The root cause, so I can take the right action (call customs, chase the bank, escalate VAT).

J3

Know how much is actually available to deploy right now — across all currencies — without needing to convert and sum manually.

J4

Initiate or approve a payout with confidence that it won't fail due to a compliance issue I didn't know about.

03 Design Process

From 14 tabs
to one question

The design process was non-linear but followed a clear principle: every screen should answer one question first. In this case, that question was: "Where is my money stuck right now?" Everything else — currency breakdowns, shipment details, tax compliance — existed in service of that question, not alongside it.

Week 4–5
Information Architecture & Concept Framing
Tested 3 organizational models with 4 users: (1) By currency, (2) By transaction status, (3) By urgency/action required. Model 3 won unanimously — users recognized their own mental model immediately. "This is exactly how I think about it."
Week 5–6
Low-Fidelity Wireframes (3 Concepts)
Explored: dashboard-style overview, linear ledger view, and the three-state triage layout (Blocked / Pending / Available). Rapid paper prototyping first, then Figma lo-fi. The three-state concept reduced cognitive load significantly in think-aloud sessions.
Week 7–9
Mid-Fidelity Prototype & Usability Testing (Round 1)
6 participants. Tested: time-to-identify most urgent block, ability to trace root cause, confidence in "Available" total. Found: users were uncertain about the distinction between Pending and Blocked — collapsed into a single "action required" vs "no action needed" model in next iteration.
Week 10–11
High-Fidelity Design & Second Usability Round
Refined the alert banner, introduced the "% of float blocked" KPI as an escalation threshold, added per-shipment "days stuck" column after users specifically asked "how long has this been sitting?". Second round: 6 new participants, 91% task completion.
Week 12–14
Engineering Handoff, QA, & Launch
Built a component-level Figma library with documented states. Ran 2 QA cycles. Shipped to EMEA enterprise beta users (14 accounts) on Feb 10, 2026. Full release Feb 24, 2026.
Pivotal Design Moment

In Round 1 testing, a user looked at the "Pending" column and said "Okay but what do I actually do with that?" — the answer was "nothing right now." That insight led to the core IA reframe: the primary split isn't Blocked / Pending / Available, it's Act Now / Will Clear / Safe to Use. The labels changed, but more importantly the hierarchy changed — Blocked got a persistent alert banner above the fold.

04 Design Decisions

What we chose,
and why

Each major design decision was contested, tested, and deliberate. The following captures the decisions that most shaped the final product — including the ones that were iterated or dropped.

Decision 1 — Three-state triage layout vs. multi-tab dashboard
KEPT
The decision

Organize the primary view around three money states — Blocked, Pending, Available — displayed in a single horizontal triptych, not a tab-nav dashboard with separate pages per category.

Why it was right

Users needed spatial comparison, not navigation. Seeing all three states simultaneously made the proportion legible — 30% blocked immediately felt different from 5% blocked. Tabs would have hidden this ratio, requiring mental arithmetic.

Decision 2 — Persistent alert banner above the fold
KEPT
The decision

A non-dismissable alert banner that names the specific blocked issues, total exposure, and a hard deadline — appears on every load when blocked items exist.

Why it was right

Finance managers open this dashboard during morning stand-up or EOD review. They need to know in 5 seconds if there's a fire. The banner makes it impossible to miss. Dismissable banners were tested — users dismissed them and then forgot the issue existed.

Decision 3 — "% of float blocked" as primary KPI
ITERATED
Original approach

Show the raw dollar value of blocked funds. Engineering team's initial instinct: "just show the number, it's clearest."

Why we changed it

$9,840 blocked feels different for a $50k business vs. a $500k business. The percentage — with a visible threshold (target: <10%) — gave context and created a shared team benchmark. Users responded to the threshold: "Oh, 30% — that's clearly a problem."

Decision 4 — Light editorial theme for an enterprise finance tool
KEPT
The design choice

Warm off-white backgrounds, serif typography for headings, monospace for all financial figures. Deliberately not dark mode — not a "terminal." Not blue/corporate. Legible, trustworthy, serious.

The rationale

Enterprise finance tools default to navy, grey, and dense grids. This created a visual language of clarity and precision — the same qualities finance users bring to their work. The serif typography signalled permanence and authority; monospace for figures ensured column alignment and digit legibility.

Decision 5 — "Days stuck" column in shipment table
KEPT
Origin

Not in the original design. Added after a user in testing said: "I need to know how long this has been sitting — that tells me if it's becoming a real problem or just processing."

Why it earned its column

Duration is how finance teams triage escalation. 1 day in customs is normal; 11 days means document dispute. The column created an implicit urgency ranking without requiring a separate sort/filter action. Color-coded: red for >7 days, amber for 3–6, grey for normal.

05 Tradeoffs

What we gave up,
and what we protected

Good product design requires explicit tradeoffs — choices to not do something in order to do something else exceptionally well. These were the hardest calls on this project.

Tradeoff Area What We Could Have Done What We Chose The Reasoning
Scope All 14 currency markets, full global view 3 currencies, 1 region (EMEA) Breadth would have made the IA unmanageable and delayed launch by 3+ months. EMEA was the highest-pain market. Solving one region excellently builds the pattern for others.
Chart / Visualization Revenue trend charts, historical graphs, sparklines No historical charts in v1 Charts answer "how did we get here?" — not "what do I do right now?" Every pixel of real estate was allocated to the operational present, not the analytical past. Charts are v2.
Currency Conversion Live FX rates with conversion toggle Static approximate USD equivalents Live FX APIs added integration complexity and latency risk. Finance teams don't make conversion decisions from a dashboard — they use treasury tools. Approximate equivalents served the "rough total" mental model without false precision.
Notification System Email/Slack alerts for new holds or blocks In-product only (persistent banner) Push notifications require notification preferences, unsubscribe flows, and delivery infrastructure. The highest-value moment was the dashboard itself — designing the alert within the product ensured it was seen in context, when the user could act on it.
Mobile Responsive mobile layout Desktop-first only (v1) Research showed 94% of reconciliation tasks were performed at a desk. Enterprise finance work requires table density and multi-column views that compress poorly on mobile. Mobile is a meaningful v2 investment — but not at the cost of desktop density and clarity.
Tax Filing UI In-product VAT filing wizard Status visibility only (link to external tool) VAT filing is a deep, regulation-specific workflow. Building it correctly would have required a separate 6-month project. Showing the status and urgency of filings — and surfacing when they were overdue — was the actual problem. Linking to the specialist tool was the right seam.
Design Philosophy Behind These Tradeoffs

The product manager pushed hard for the global 14-currency view in v1. The argument: "our biggest accounts need it." My counter: a product that does one thing perfectly builds more trust than a product that does many things adequately. Shipping a focused, coherent v1 gave us the user credibility to earn the scope expansion in v2.

06 Before & After

The workflow
that changed

The clearest measure of design impact is the before/after comparison of how a user completes their core job. For this finance team, that job was the Monday morning liquidity check — the first task every week.

~110 minutes total to complete full liquidity review

📋
Log into bank portal for each of 3 currencies → check pending holds → manual note in spreadsheet
📋
Check VAT compliance SaaS → note outstanding filings → cross-reference with payment calendar
📋
Sum USD equivalents manually (approximate FX from Google)
⚠️
Discover 2 customs holds and an overdue VAT reclaim — 11 days after they started
📧

AFTER — Monday Morning Workflow
⏱
~12 minutes total including payout approval actions
✓
Open dashboard → alert banner surfaces 2 customs holds + overdue VAT in 5 seconds
✓
Three-state view shows $9,840 blocked (30.6% of float) — immediately above threshold
✓
Shipment table shows #ORD-4481 has been held 11 days — escalate to customs broker
✓
Currency wallets show $33,540 available — confirm payout to Nordic Supply can proceed
✓
Review & approve 3 pending payouts with one action
✓

The workflow compression wasn't just about speed — it was about confidence. Before the redesign, finance managers described the Monday review as stressful: "You know there's something wrong, you just have to find it." After launch, the first user feedback session produced this note unprompted from a participant: "I actually feel like I know what's going on now."

07 Outcome

Measurable results
30 days post-launch

The dashboard shipped to 14 EMEA enterprise accounts on February 10, 2026, with a full release on February 24. The following metrics were collected from product analytics, support ticket analysis, and a 30-day post-launch survey (n=18 finance team members across 8 accounts).

73%
Reduction in identification time
Average time to identify the most urgent blocked payment dropped from 47 minutes (spreadsheet workflow) to 12 minutes (dashboard). Measured via session recordings + self-reported timing in post-launch survey.
2.4×
Faster payout initiation
Time from "decide to pay out" to "payout approved" reduced by 2.4× across tracked accounts. Baseline measured via support ticket timestamps; post-launch via product analytics event tracking.
91%
Usability test task completion
In formal usability testing (n=12, unmoderated via Maze), 91% of participants completed the 3 core tasks without assistance or backtracking on first use. Prior tool averaged 61%.
0
"Where is my money" support tickets
The highest-volume support ticket category pre-launch. Zero in the first 30 days post-launch across 14 accounts. Not a fluke — users now self-serve the answer in the product.
4.4/5
Post-launch satisfaction score
30-day NPS survey of active users (n=18). Top-rated attributes: "clarity of what needs action" (4.7), "trust in the numbers shown" (4.5). Lowest: "currency coverage" (3.6) — confirming v2 priority.
3.6/5
Currency coverage satisfaction
The deliberate v1 scope tradeoff (3 currencies, EMEA only) is the top-rated improvement request. This validates the tradeoff decision — it confirmed demand for expansion without compromising v1 quality.
Unintended Positive Outcome

Two accounts reported using the "% of float blocked" threshold (target <10%) as a weekly KPI in their finance stand-up. This wasn't designed as a reporting metric — it emerged as a shared language between the finance team and their CFO. A designed artefact becoming an organizational communication tool is a strong signal of product-market fit at the workflow layer.

What I'd Do Differently

↻

Involve engineering earlier in IA exploration. The three-state layout required a specific data aggregation model that created back-end complexity we didn't anticipate until week 8. Earlier collaboration would have shaped the IA with implementation constraints in mind from week 4.

↻

Test the alert banner with non-urgent states. The persistent banner was designed for the "there is a problem" state. We didn't adequately design the "everything is fine" state — the first 3 users who logged in with no blocked funds were momentarily confused by the absence of the banner. A clear "All clear — 0 items blocked" state was needed.

↻

Commission a proper accessibility audit earlier. Color coding (red/amber/green) for status was tested with 2 colorblind participants at the end of Round 2. Both relied on the text labels, which were sufficient — but the review should have happened in Round 1 to avoid the risk of a late-stage redesign.

What's Next (v2 Roadmap)

Confirmed for v2

Multi-currency expansion (6 currencies, 3 regions). Push notifications for new holds. Historical trend view (last 90 days). Mobile-responsive layout for approval workflows on the go.

Under Evaluation

Predictive hold flagging (ML-based, using historical customs pattern data). VAT filing integration (partnership model). Team roles — read-only analyst view vs. approver view.
